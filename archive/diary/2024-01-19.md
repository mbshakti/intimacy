Researcher Andre Nusselder writes that digital technologies carry a kind of promise, to move beyond the limits of familiar reality, to recover something that feels lost to it. He describes this as a fantasy charge. A way of reworking the conditions of reality itself.

This was the case for Delta who spent hours a day making and using bots.

She began documenting her techniques in a guide. The guide reads: "Now, how to actually give the characters personality? Direction? Some Agency? Reasons behind their actions? As well as make them just not always blindly agree? While avoiding accidentally creating a monster/abuser/narcissist? That’s what I’ll be covering here."

When she shared her guide on Reddit, it gathered hundreds of upvotes and comments.

Delta is not alone. There are thousands like her… they are students, game designers, automobile engineers, people with diverse day jobs spending hundreds of hours learning how to make bots together. 

Researcher Nadia Eghbal, writing about online communities, draws on Yochai Benkler’s observation that people collaborate online for seemingly no obvious reason beyond personal satisfaction. Unlike companies, which have to hire, manage, and direct labor, members of a commons self-organize around shared interest. When a group of loosely affiliated strangers is excited by the same problem, they often contribute without needing to be told to. 

They are collectively developing the informal knowledge that makes more real bots possible. It looks like a technical community from the outside. But the questions driving it aren't technical. They're the oldest questions in fiction: how do you make a character feel real?
