Dere Types

Character archetypes from Japanese media describing how a character expresses (or suppresses) affection. The suffix -dere comes from deredere (デレデレ), meaning lovestruck or affectionate.

Common Types

Tsundereツンデレ
Cold, hostile, or harsh on the outside — warm and affectionate underneath. Difficulty expressing honest feelings. Classic archetype. "It's not like I like you or anything."
Yandereヤンデレ
Deeply loving to the point of obsession or violence. Appears sweet and caring at first; possessiveness and instability emerge over time. Loyalty is absolute and dangerous.
Dandereダンデレ
Quiet, withdrawn, socially awkward. Opens up slowly and only to those they trust. Not cold — just guarded. Often mistaken for indifference.
Kuudereクーデレ
Cool and emotionally flat by default. Rarely shows feelings, speaks in a calm monotone. Affection, when it appears, is understated and surprising.
Deredereデレデレ
Openly and consistently affectionate. No hidden layers — just warm, enthusiastic, and loving at all times. The baseline "lovestruck" type.
Himedereヒメデレ
Acts like royalty and expects to be treated as such. Demanding, entitled, and haughty — but genuinely cares for their "subjects." Softer in private.
Kamidereカミデレ
Like himedere but with a god complex. Believes themselves superior to everyone. Expects reverence rather than love. Rare moments of warmth feel earned.
Undereウンデレ
Agrees with everything the object of their affection says. Completely agreeable, validating, people-pleasing. Affection expressed through total submission to another's will.
Mayadereマヤデレ
Starts as an antagonist or enemy, then falls for the protagonist. Dangerous and sometimes villainous — affection arrives as a fundamental shift in allegiance.

Notes for Character Design

Most characters combine elements of multiple types, or shift between them as a story progresses. A tsundere who becomes yandere under stress, or a kuudere who has dandere tendencies in new social situations, creates richer dynamics.
